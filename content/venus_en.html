VENUS: THE FIERY TWIN SISTER

Venus, the second planet from the Sun, is often called Earth's "twin sister" due to its similar size, mass, and composition. However, its surface is a true hellscape, with scorching temperatures, crushing atmospheric pressure, and thick clouds of sulfuric acid. Despite being farther from the Sun than Mercury, Venus is the hottest planet in the Solar System.

Key Features

  • Hottest planet: Average surface temperatures reach around 465°C (869°F), hot enough to melt lead. This is caused by an extreme greenhouse effect due to its thick carbon dioxide-rich atmosphere.
  • Thick atmosphere: The atmospheric pressure on Venus is over 90 times that of Earth's, similar to being 1 km underwater on Earth. It's composed mainly of CO₂ (96.5%) and nitrogen (3.5%).
  • Sulfuric acid clouds: Venus is shrouded in dense, opaque clouds made of sulfuric acid droplets. These clouds reflect sunlight strongly, making Venus one of the brightest objects in the sky after the Sun and Moon.
  • Retrograde rotation: Venus spins very slowly and in the opposite direction of most planets in the Solar System. A day on Venus (243 Earth days) is longer than its year (225 Earth days).
  • No moons: Venus and Mercury are the only planets in the Solar System without natural satellites.
  • Volcanic terrain: The surface is dominated by vast volcanic plains and thousands of volcanoes, including massive shield volcanoes like Maat Mons. There is evidence that volcanic activity may still be ongoing.
  • Maxwell Montes: The tallest mountain range on Venus, with peaks reaching up to 11 km above the surrounding plains.

Runaway Greenhouse Effect: Venus's dense CO₂ atmosphere has trapped solar heat, causing extreme surface temperatures. It serves as a dramatic example of a "runaway greenhouse effect," offering a cautionary tale for Earth's climate if greenhouse gases are not controlled.

Planetary Structure

Like Earth, Venus is believed to have a layered internal structure:

  • Core: A metallic iron core, possibly partially molten, with a radius of about 3,200 km.
  • Mantle: A hot silicate rock mantle about 2,800 km thick.
  • Crust: A relatively thin silicate rock crust, estimated to be 10–30 km thick.

However, due to the lack of plate tectonics, how Venus releases internal heat remains an active area of research.

Exploration History

Venus was one of the first targets of human space exploration due to its proximity to Earth.

  • Venera Program (Soviet Union): A series of successful missions, including orbiters and landers. Venera 7 became the first spacecraft to land successfully on another planet (1970), and later missions returned the first images from Venus's hellish surface.
  • Pioneer Venus (NASA): Included an orbiter and multiple atmospheric probes that provided detailed data on Venus's atmosphere and surface.
  • Magellan (NASA): Used radar mapping to penetrate Venus's thick clouds and mapped 98% of the surface with high resolution, revealing complex volcanic features.
  • Venus Express (ESA): An orbiter that studied Venus’s atmosphere in detail.
  • Akatsuki (JAXA): Japan's current orbiter, still operating, focused on the dynamics of Venus's atmosphere.

Several future missions are planned, including NASA's VERITAS and DAVINCI+, aiming to uncover more about Venus’s geological history, atmosphere, and the possibility that it once hosted oceans.
