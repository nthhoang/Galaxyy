THE SUN: THE HEART OF THE SOLAR SYSTEM

The Sun is the star at the center of our Solar System, a massive burning ball of plasma that illuminates and warms the Earth, enabling life to exist and thrive. It accounts for 99.86% of the total mass of the entire Solar System and is the primary energy source for most natural processes on our planet.

Basic Characteristics

  • Type: G-type main-sequence star (G2V), often referred to as a "yellow dwarf."
  • Age: Approximately 4.6 billion years.
  • Diameter: About 1.39 million km (109 times Earth).
  • Mass: About 333,000 times that of Earth.
  • Main Composition: Mostly Hydrogen (~74%) and Helium (~24%), with small amounts of heavier elements.
  • Surface Temperature (Photosphere): Around 5,500°C.
  • Core Temperature: Estimated up to 15 million °C.

Structure of the Sun

The Sun has a layered structure, from inside out including:

  • Core: Where nuclear fusion occurs, converting Hydrogen into Helium and releasing immense energy. The pressure and temperature here are extremely high.
  • Radiative Zone: Energy from the core is transported outward via light photons (radiation). These photons take a very long time (up to hundreds of thousands of years) to pass through due to constant absorption and re-emission.
  • Convective Zone: Energy is primarily transported by convection—hot plasma rises and cooler plasma sinks, similar to boiling water.
  • Photosphere: The visible surface layer of the Sun, where most of the sunlight we receive is emitted. Sunspots also appear here.
  • Chromosphere: A layer of the atmosphere just above the photosphere, with a distinct red color, best seen during total solar eclipses or with specialized instruments.
  • Corona: The outermost and thinnest part of the Sun’s atmosphere, stretching millions of kilometers into space. It has an extremely high temperature, up to several million °C, and is visible during total solar eclipses.

How long does sunlight take to reach Earth? With an average distance of 149.6 million km and a light speed of about 299,792 km/s, sunlight takes approximately 8 minutes and 20 seconds to reach our planet.

Sun’s Activities

The Sun is a very dynamic star with many fascinating phenomena:

  • Sunspots: Temporary dark areas on the photosphere with lower temperatures and strong magnetic fields. Their number changes in an 11-year cycle.
  • Solar Flares: Powerful bursts of energy from the Sun’s surface, releasing electromagnetic radiation across the spectrum.
  • Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs): Huge clouds of plasma and magnetic fields expelled from the corona. When directed at Earth, they can cause geomagnetic storms.
  • Solar Wind: A continuous stream of charged particles (mostly protons and electrons) flowing out from the Sun into space.
  • Prominences: Large, bright arc-shaped plasma structures extending from the Sun’s surface to the corona, held by magnetic fields.

Energy Source and Life Cycle

The Sun’s energy comes from nuclear fusion in its core, where Hydrogen atoms fuse into Helium, releasing enormous energy as described by Einstein’s famous equation E=mc².

The Sun is currently in the middle of its life cycle (main sequence star). In about 5 billion years, when the core’s Hydrogen is depleted, it will expand into a red giant star, potentially engulfing the inner planets including Earth. Eventually, it will shed its outer layers, creating a planetary nebula, leaving behind a dense core called a white dwarf.

Importance and Exploration

The Sun not only provides the light and warmth necessary for life on Earth but also influences weather, climate, and ocean currents. Studying the Sun helps us better understand stars, magnetic fields, and their impact on space near Earth (space weather).

  • SOHO (Solar and Heliospheric Observatory): A space observatory by ESA and NASA, providing valuable data on the Sun since 1995.
  • Parker Solar Probe (NASA): Launched in 2018, this spacecraft is on a mission to "touch the Sun" by flying through the corona at an extremely close distance.
  • Solar Orbiter (ESA/NASA): Launched in 2020, it is studying the Sun and its poles from new perspectives.
